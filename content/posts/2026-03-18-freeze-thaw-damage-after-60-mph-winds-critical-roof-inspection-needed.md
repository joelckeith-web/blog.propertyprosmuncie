## What the 60 MPH Winds Did to Muncie Roofs

The severe winds that swept through our area over the past 48 hours have left a trail of hidden and visible damage across Muncie neighborhoods. Our [roofing inspection teams](https://www.propertyprosmuncie.com/roof-inspections/) have documented consistent patterns of damage throughout Delaware County:

**Shingle Displacement and Lifting**: Sustained winds above 45 MPH begin lifting shingle edges, and our 60 MPH gusts were more than sufficient to compromise seal integrity. Even if shingles appear intact from ground level, the adhesive strips may be compromised, creating vulnerable points for water intrusion.

**Flashing Damage**: Metal flashing around chimneys, vents, and roof valleys is particularly susceptible to high winds. We've found numerous instances where flashing has been bent, lifted, or completely displaced, creating direct pathways for water infiltration.

**Granule Loss**: High winds strip protective granules from asphalt shingles. If you notice an unusual amount of granules in your gutters or around your home's perimeter, this indicates surface damage that reduces your roof's weather resistance.

**Structural Stress Points**: The wind loading from 60 MPH gusts creates stress at connection points between roofing materials and structural elements. This stress can manifest as loose nails, separated joints, or micro-fractures that aren't immediately visible.

> **Key Takeaway**: Wind damage often creates small vulnerabilities that become major problems when exposed to freeze-thaw cycles. What seems like minor damage today can become catastrophic leaks when water freezes and expands in compromised areas.

## The Immediate Threat: Assessing Current Damage

Before addressing the incoming weather threat, every Muncie homeowner needs to understand what damage may already exist. Our [professional roof inspection services](https://www.propertyprosmuncie.com/roof-inspections/) focus on these critical areas after wind events:

**Exterior Visual Assessment**: From ground level, use binoculars to scan for obvious damage signs. Look for missing shingles, exposed underlayment, bent gutters, or debris accumulation. Never attempt to climb on a potentially damaged roof – leave that to certified professionals.

**Interior Inspection Protocol**: Check your attic space for new water stains, dampness, or daylight visible through the roof structure. Pay special attention to areas around chimneys, vents, and where different roof planes meet. Fresh water damage appears as dark stains or drip marks on rafters or insulation.

**Gutter System Evaluation**: High winds often fill gutters with debris, creating ice dam conditions when temperatures fluctuate. Clogged gutters prevent proper drainage and can cause water to back up under roofing materials during freeze-thaw cycles.

**Siding and Trim Connections**: Wind damage to [siding](https://www.propertyprosmuncie.com/siding/) often occurs where rooflines meet walls. Check these intersection points for gaps or damage that could allow water penetration during temperature swings.

The National Roofing Contractors Association (NRCA) emphasizes that [post-storm inspections should occur within 72 hours](https://www.nrca.net) of severe weather events to prevent secondary damage from developing.

**The Science of Freeze-Thaw Damage**: When water enters even small gaps in roofing materials and then freezes, it expands by approximately 9%. This expansion force can widen existing cracks, separate flashing joints, and push shingles apart. When temperatures rise and the ice melts, larger gaps remain for the next freeze cycle to exploit.

**Compound Effect on Wind Damage**: Areas where 60 MPH winds have already loosened materials become exponentially more vulnerable. A slightly lifted shingle edge becomes a major water entry point after just one freeze-thaw cycle. What might have been a simple [roof repair](https://www.propertyprosmuncie.com/roof-repairs/) on Monday could require extensive replacement work by Friday.
